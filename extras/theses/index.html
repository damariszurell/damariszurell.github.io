Intra-specific variation in dispersal: a simulation approach (Master thesis)

Dispersal is a central biological process determining the movement and genetic exchange between populations, and the range dynamics of species under global change. Often dispersal is described in form of empirical dispersal kernels that summarise the observed dispersal distances over many individuals of a species. Thereby, it is important to acknowledge that species or population-level dispersal kernels are an emergent property resulting from complex interplay between movement capacity of individuals, behaviour and environment. As changes in landscape configuration affect the cost of movement, we can expect that population-level dispersal kernels vary between different landscapes.

In this project, we will use the individual-based modelling platform RangeShifter (and the associated R package RangeShiftR) to simulate dispersal of individuals in landscapes with different degrees of fragmentation. The thesis could focus on different aspects of dispersal, for example:

  1. Comparison of emergent population-level dispersal kernels across different landscapes
  2. Evolution of dispersal traits in landscapes with different degrees of fragmentation

Starting date: flexible.

Prerequisites: Good understanding of R and of individual-based modelling, statistical analyses in R.

Contact: Prof. Dr. Damaris Zurell

Dynamic occupancy modelling of Swiss breeding birds (Master thesis)

Predictions of current and future species range dynamics and population trends are prerequisite for designing adequate management strategies. Dynamic occupancy models describe temporal changes in speciesâ€™ occupancy based on the relationships between colonisation and extinction probabilities and environment. These are typically estimated from time series of occurrence data. Dynamic occupancy models show great promise for global change research by providing a comparably simple data-driven yet process-explicit modelling approach. Yet, several open questions remain for example regarding robust and efficient strategies for variable selection and model comparison.

In this project, we will analyse the occupancy dynamics of selected Swiss breeding birds for which time series of occurrence data are available since 1999. Dynamic occupancy models will be fit using maximum likelihood as well as Bayesian methods. The thesis could focus on different aspects of model building or species range dynamics, for example:

  1. Variable selection strategies of adequate environmental predictor variables
  2. Multi-model comparison for attributing the relative impact of climate and land use change on observed occupancy dynamics

Starting date: flexible.

Prerequisites: Good understanding of R, statistical analyses in R, initial understanding of Bayesian statistics advantageous.

Contact: Prof. Dr. Damaris Zurell

Climate change-induced niche shifts in North American birds (Master thesis)

Over recent decades we have observed complex range shifts in diverse species groups in response to global change. As suitable climatic conditions are shifting due to global warming, so do the species. Yet, observations are often not as clear-cut and observed range shifts are slower or in a different direction than expected from the climate trajectories alone. Different reasons might be responsible for these discrepancies, including different global change drivers such as land use change, but also ecological factors such as novel or missing interspecific interactions, and adaptations.

The aim of this Master thesis is to disentangle these factors by comparing range shifts in geographic space versus niche shifts in environmental space. As case study, you will focus on the North American Breeding Bird Survey with annual bird counts since 1970. You will analyse whether geographic range shifts in different bird species were accompanied by niche shifts in environmental space, by niche contraction (as proxy for dispersal limitation) or by niche expansion (as proxy for adaptation and competitor release).

Starting date: flexible.

Prerequisites: Good understanding of R, data handling and analyses in R. Experience with spatial data in R and macroecological analyses.

Contact: Prof. Dr. Damaris Zurell

Climatic niche asymmetry in plants of the Americas (Master thesis)

It is generally assumed that abiotic stress for a species increases towards the cold end of its climatic niche whereas biotic stress increases towards the warm end of its climate niche. Correspondingly, the asymmetric abiotic stress limitation hypothesis predicts that asymmetry in the realised niche of species should be highest in high latitude and high altitude species.

In this thesis, you will test for abiotic stress, in particular temperature stress, in plant species of the Americas. Distribution data are available from BIEN (Botanical Information and Ecology Network). You will fit species distribution models for different plant species and assess niche asymmetry. Niche shapes will be related to plant functional and geographic traits.

Starting date: flexible.

Prerequisites: Good understanding of R, data handling and analyses in R. Experience with spatial data in R, macroecological analyses and species distribution modelling.

Contact: Prof. Dr. Damaris Zurell
